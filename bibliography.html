Bibliography

  • Blalock, G., DeVaro, J. L., Leventhal, S., & Simon, D. H. (2007). Gender Bias in Power Relationships: Evidence from Police Traffic Stops. SSRN Electronic Journal. https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.998464

    In the article Gender bias in power relationships: evidence from police traffic stops, Blalock et al. examines whether police officers are less likely to give traffic tickets to men or women during traffic stops by analyzing traffic violations in five different regions in the United States. The conventional assumption is that women are less likely to be issued tickets, however, the results of the study reveal otherwise. The examinations concluded that there is no true gender bias in the issuing of tickets, but the regional location may impact the likelihood of citations. For example, the study found that women are more likely to receive a ticket in three of the five locations examined, whereas men were more likely to receive a ticket in the other two. This information will be helpful in our analyses of how race and gender affect whether a warning or ticket is given during a traffic stop.

    This Montgomery County news article uncovers a recent incident of a cop screaming at and threatening a five year old black boy. This source will be helpful in our first three paragraphs when we explain why Montgomery County Police data is worth looking into.

  • Brame, Robert, et al. “Demographic Patterns of Cumulative Arrest Prevalence by Ages 18 and 23.” Crime and Delinquency, vol. 60, no. 3, SAGE Publications, 2014, pp. 471–86, https://doi.org/10.1177/0011128713514801.

    In the article Demographic Patterns of Cumulative Arrest Prevalence by Ages 18-23, Robert Brame (professor in the Department of Criminology and Criminal Justice at the University of South Carolina) along with many of his colleagues analyze race, sex, and self-reported histories from the National Longitudinal Survey of Youth starting in the year 1997 and ending in 2008. With their main focus of attempting to describe and study what can be learned about race and sex differences in the cumulative risk of arrest by ages 18 to 23, their analyze yeilded many very important findings: (a) males have a higher cumulative prevalence of arrest then females, (b) there are important racial differences in the probability of arrest for males but not for females, and mainly that the risk of arrest is not evenly distributed across the population. This kind of the research is incredibly useful for our project because it touches many of our research questions regarding racial profiling as well as how arrests for minorities compare with white arrests.

  • Chenane, J. L., Wright, E. M., & Gibson, C. L. (2019). Traffic stops, race, and perceptions of fairness. Policing and Society, 30(6), 720–737. https://doi.org/10.1080/10439463.2019.1587436

    In the article Traffic Stops, Race, and Perceptions of Fairness, Joselyne Chenane (Criminology and Justice Studies, University of Massachusetts) along with her colleagues ask and the study the questions regarding whether or not pos/neg interaction with the police through traffic stops affect how people contact the police in non-crime/emergency situations. As well as why perceptions of fairness and reasons for traffic stops might affect these outcomes across different racial/enthic categories. Their results yielded that citizens stopped for traffic violations and especially those who perceived unfair treatment are significantly less likely to seek help from the police and/or report non-crime and emergency situations. This article is useful for our research because not only does it address and analyze the unfair treatment in which many minorities face when it comes to the police, but it also examines how that unfairness goes on to influence future choices long-term when it comes to law enforcement.

  • Davis. (1997). Race, cops, and traffic stops. University of Miami Law Review, 51(2), 425.

    Angela J. Davis, professor of law at the American University's Washington College of Law, is a specialist in criminal law with a focus on prosecutorial power and racism in the criminal justice system. In the Article Race, Cops, and Traffic Stops, Davis discusses the Supreme Courts negligence in providing a clear and effective solution for discriminatory pretextual traffic stops. Her discussion of the “discretionary nature of pretextual traffic stops and their discriminatory effect on African-Americans and Latinos”, along with her examination of realted court cases, will be helpful in our analysis of warnings, searches, and arrests based on race and gender.

  • E. Pierson, C. Simoiu, J. Overgoor, S. Corbett-Davies, D. Jenson, A. Shoemaker, V. Ramachandran, P. Barghouty, C. Phillips, R. Shroff, and S. Goel. “A large-scale analysis of racial disparities in police stops across the United States”. Nature Human Behaviour, Vol. 4, 2020.

    A large-scale analysis of racial disparities in police stops across the United States by E. Piereson et al. studies a multitude of datasets to examine situations where race may play a factor in police stops. Such as if more black drivers are stopped during the day as opposed to night where it is harder for the officer to determine a driver's race, whether or not the decision to search drivers for contraband has any bias, as well as if drug policy has an effect of these outcomes (States with or without legalized recreational marijuana). The datasets were collected by filing public record requests with each state's state patrol as well as several municipal police departments. Both the data & analysis in this paper helps guide the research of Montgomery County's data as well as how to digitally manage Montgomery County's data.

  • ROJEK, J., ROSENFELD, R., & DECKER, S. (2012). POLICING RACE: THE RACIAL STRATIFICATION OF SEARCHES IN POLICE TRAFFIC STOPS*. Criminology, 50(4), 993–1024. https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1745-9125.2012.00285.x

    In the article Policing Race: The Racial Stratification of Searches in Police Traffic Stops, Jeff Rojek (Department of Criminology and Criminal Justice, University of South Carolina) along with his colleagues examine propositions from Donald Black’s theory of law to explain the interaction effects of officer and driver race on searches in traffic stops located in St. Louis, Missouri. Their results are as follows: (a) Searches are more likely in stops of Black drivers than in those of White drivers, especially by White officers; (b) In predominantly Black communities, stops of White drivers by White officers are most likely to result in a search. Rojek and his team understand both of these findings to be results of manifestations of racial profiling in segregated communities. This article is useful for our research because not only does it talk about race and the probability of stops and/or searches, but it also examines the racial identity of the officers at hand.

  • Rowe, B. (2008). Gender Bias in the Enforcement of Traffic Laws: Evidence Based on a New Empirical Test. SSRN Electronic Journal. https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.1120602

    In the article Gender Bias in the Enforcement of Traffic Laws: Evidence Based on a New Empirical Test, Rowe conducts an empirical test that considers police gender bias and is aimed at discovering whether the gender disparity in traffic tickets results from gender bias or a higher tendency of male drivers to break traffic laws. This article will be helpful in establishing context for our analysis and argument.

  • Tillyer, R., & Engel, R. S. (2010). The Impact of Drivers’ Race, Gender, and Age During Traffic Stops. Crime & Delinquency, 59(3), 369–395. https://doi.org/10.1177/0011128710389583

    In the article The Impact of Drivers’ Race, Gender, and Age During Traffic Stops: Assessing Interaction Terms and the Social Conditioning Model Tillyer and Engel examine the relationship between specific combinations of driver demographics and traffic stop outcomes, as well as investigate research questions based on the social conditioning model. The results found that warnings and citations were disproportionately issued to young, Black, male drivers, and that legal factors influence police decision making during traffic stops. This information will come in handy when exploring how race and gender affect warnings, searches, and arrests at traffic stops.
